1/8
I built a Chinese digital exhibition called Who Holds the Camera? China Through Other Lenses.

It looks at photography, power, memory, and how "China" has been visually formed through Western-facing image trails.

2/8
This is not a nostalgia photo grid, and not a Chinese edition of Flashbak.

Secondary visual pages are treated as leads. The project keeps tracing objects back toward archives, museums, photographer projects, galleries, university collections, and public-domain records.

3/8
The site is structured as an online exhibition:

Entrance, curator's statement, gallery map, exhibition routes, 9 gallery overview sections, 9 dedicated gallery pages, object labels, looking questions, archive table, and source index.

The home page now works as an overview exhibition instead of laying every object into one long grid.

4/8
Each object includes a source trail:

title, year, creator, place, medium, credit, source links, archive or project links, rights note, captions, curatorial note, tags, and source status.

5/8
The important design decision was to make uncertainty visible.

The interface shows verified archive source, rights unclear, secondary source only, and placeholder pending replacement as explicit status badges.

6/8
Current scope:

- 9 galleries

- 5 exhibition routes
- 5 thematic trails
- Lightbox viewing mode with previous / next
- Archive Drawer sections

7/8
This is also an AI-assisted curation experiment.

AI helped with structure, data modeling, copy drafts, and static site implementation. But source transparency and rights uncertainty had to be designed as part of the product.
